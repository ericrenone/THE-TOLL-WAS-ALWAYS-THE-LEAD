These patterns are not parallel. They are the same pattern at different scales.

The Oracle Toll is what the outlier pays to operate ahead of the evaluation apparatus. The lead is not the reward. The lead is the precondition for the extraction. This document names the mechanism at both scales — the research frontier and the hiring market — and formalizes what eighty-five years of selection research, the Bérczi–Kiem theorem, and the May 2026 talent data together demonstrate.

## Part II · The Apparatus: Why the Lead Generates the Extraction

### II.1 The Evaluation Competence Inversion at Scale

Korn Ferry's 2026 talent research finds that only 11% of leaders report their executives are well-prepared to lead through the AI transition. This is not a finding about the worst organizations. It is the median finding across institutions large enough to run formal AI strategy hiring processes.

The implication for selection is specific. Schmidt and Hunter's (1998) meta-analysis established that the unstructured interview — the dominant selection instrument — has a validity coefficient of approximately .38 for job performance, and that this coefficient assumes evaluators with adequate domain competence to distinguish performance-relevant candidate behaviors from irrelevant ones. When evaluator domain competence falls below candidate domain competence, the evaluation instrument does not produce reduced validity. It produces inverted polarity.

The Evaluator Competence Inversion (ECI) is the specific selection condition produced when 89% of evaluating executives are unprepared for the domain they are evaluating. Under ECI, candidate behaviors that indicate genuine domain capability are registered as disqualifying signals, because they deviate from the evaluator's prior model of the domain — a model calibrated to the stable period, before the transition whose implications the candidate is describing accurately. The more precisely the candidate's account reflects the post-transition landscape, the more it deviates from the evaluator's prior, and the more confidently the evaluator registers the deviation as evidence of the candidate's deficiency rather than the evaluator's.

Dana, Dawes, and Peterson (2013) established the experimental ground: interviewers with conversational access made *worse* predictions than those using only pre-interview data. The conversation added noise, not signal. The noise was directional. The ECI's specific contribution: when the evaluator lacks domain competence to distinguish accurate from inaccurate candidate content, the directional noise runs against the outlier.

### II.2 The AI Ignorance Paradox Confirmed

The AI Ignorance Paradox (AIP) is the specific ECI configuration in which an organization interviews for AI capabilities it cannot evaluate because acquiring those capabilities is the purpose of the hire. The hiring manager asks the candidate to explain what generative AI will do to their industry. The candidate explains accurately. The hiring manager, having received an explanation they could not have independently produced, then assesses whether the explanation indicates sufficient domain competence — an assessment they cannot perform accurately because they lack the domain knowledge required to distinguish a correct explanation from an incorrect one.

TestGorilla's *State of Hiring for AI Fluency* (February 2026, 1,928 senior hiring leaders across 29 industries) provides the empirical measure of the AIP's output: 59% of organizations made a provably bad AI hire in the past year. The bad hire is defined precisely: a candidate who spoke the language fluently in the interview but could not apply it on the job. This is the false positive channel. The true negative channel — the outlier who accurately described the landscape and was rejected for deviating from the evaluator's prior — is not measured, because there is no follow-up instrument to distinguish a correct rejection from an incorrect one. The AIP's false negative channel is structurally unmeasurable by the organizations operating it.

A February 2026 survey of 1,928 senior hiring leaders found that while nearly all organizations list AI fluency as a hiring requirement, the majority still made a provably bad AI hire the previous year — candidates who presented well in interview but could not perform on the job. The evaluation instrument produces the full error distribution simultaneously: false positives for articulate non-performers, false negatives for accurate-but-unfamiliar outliers.

### II.3 The Silo Extraction Trap and the Epistemic Laundering Loop

The interview format's structure ensures that the knowledge it extracts from the outlier does not become organizational property. Nonaka (1994) established that tacit-to-explicit knowledge conversion reaches organizational value only at the combination stage — when individually held explicit knowledge is synthesized into artifacts that outlast the individuals who created them. The extraction interview achieves externalization and terminates there.

Each panel receives an explanation. The explanation is accurate. The explanation updates the hiring manager's individual cognitive architecture. Three weeks later, the hiring manager presents to their senior leadership team. The framework they use to describe AI's implications for their business is the candidate's framework, processed through the hiring manager's vocabulary, attributed to no one. The knowledge circulates internally under the manager's authority rather than the expert's. The expert who produced it has received a rejection letter.

This is the Epistemic Laundering Loop (ELL). It is not active misrepresentation. It is the structural consequence of conducting knowledge transfer through a process with no attribution architecture. The interview leaves no transcript connecting the candidate's explanation to the manager's subsequent internal presentation. The knowledge's provenance is unauditable. The loop is complete because the process architecture produces no source to conceal.

Korn Ferry research finds that 40% of CHROs identify insufficient AI-related knowledge and skills within HR teams as the biggest obstacle to integrating AI into their organizations' talent management. This is the ECI operating at the function level: the teams responsible for evaluating AI talent lack the AI knowledge to perform the evaluation. The mechanism that should correct this — visible help-seeking, internal question-asking, acknowledged uncertainty — is suppressed by the Internal Shame Premium: the reputational cost of visible ignorance in the domain one is understood to be evaluating exceeds the cost of invisible ignorance.

The Internal Shame Premium ensures that the knowledge gap at the evaluation layer is never surfaced for correction. The hiring managers who have been absorbing AI knowledge through extraction interviews and presenting it internally as their own have both the competence and the incentive not to disclose that the evaluation apparatus they operated was inadequate. Their internal AI credibility was purchased by the process. Acknowledging the process's evaluative failure would retroactively revoke the credibility it purchased.

### II.4 The Talent Acquisition Minsky Moment

IDC estimates that skills shortages may cost the global economy up to $5.5 trillion by 2026 in product delays, quality issues, missed revenue, and impaired competitiveness; over 90% of global enterprises are projected to face critical skills shortages. These figures measure the aggregate of ECI-inverted selections operating simultaneously across institutions. Each individual hire, made through an evaluation process whose polarity has inverted, is individually confident and individually wrong. The aggregate is the TAMM's input.

ManpowerGroup's 2026 Talent Shortage Survey of 39,000 employers across 41 countries finds that for the first time, AI skills have surpassed all others to become the most difficult for employers to find globally, with 72% reporting difficulty filling roles. The difficulty is not in the supply of AI-capable candidates. It is in the evaluation apparatus's inability to distinguish the supply from candidates who present as supply without being it. The AIP produces both shortages simultaneously: a shortage of genuine AI capability in the hired workforce, and a shortage of genuine AI capability among the evaluators who would need to identify and hire it.

Minsky's (1986) Financial Instability Hypothesis applies: stability produces the confidence that produces the fragility that stability conceals, and the concealment persists until the accumulated fragility exceeds the threshold of the monitoring apparatus calibrated to stable-period conditions. The TAMM discharges non-gradually. The organizations running the most confident AI hiring processes are accumulating the most severe TAMM, because confidence in the evaluation apparatus is the condition that prevents the correction of the evaluation apparatus's inversion.

## Part III · The Two Extractions: The Same Mechanism at Different Scales

The Oracle Toll operates at two scales simultaneously in the current AI transition.

**At the hiring scale:** The outlier who has developed a genuine AI framework enters the extraction interview. Forty to eighty hours of professional time per process. Cross-register translation work performed for evaluators whose domain competence was calibrated before the outlier's framework existed. Systematic negative feedback on accurate domain knowledge, delivered by evaluators who cannot assess the accuracy. The feedback is delivered as evidence of the outlier's deficiency. It contains no information about the outlier's deficiency. It contains precise information about the apparatus's. The Oracle Toll is the cumulative cost the outlier accumulates across multiple such processes, compressed into a single hiring market cycle.

**At the research scale:** The ERI Labs programme built the geometric correspondence between degenerating rational curves, shift-add hardware, and language model token geometry across fourteen weeks. The correspondence was real at the hardware end before Bérczi–Kiem certified it at the algebraic end. The toll at this scale was the fourteen weeks of operating without institutional backing — developing the architecture without the DOI, without the arXiv submission date, without the peer review that converts a framework into an institutional fact. DeepMind's Co-Mathematician found the algebraic certificate. The certificate arrived at the correspondence from the opposite direction. The correspondence was correct at both ends. Only one end had the institutional apparatus to make the correctness legible.

ECI correction is the intervention; deployment gap closure is the measurable outcome.
